Who I Am

I started working at 15 in a Bombay darkroom. No backup plan, no safety net. I dropped out after 10th standard and went straight to work — hard, unglamorous, sweaty, instructive. Everything I know about execution under pressure comes from those years.

In 2001, the Bhuj earthquake happened and I went there. The scale of suffering made it impossible to return to what I was doing before. Around the same time I started reading — every religion, every philosophy — genuinely looking for answers. I read honestly and came out an atheist. The logic wasn't there. That's still where I stand.

In 2002, Gujarat burned. I was there. I was attacked by a mob and beaten by the police. Both the street and the state failed at once. That experience didn't break me — it made me fearless. It gave me a permanent, bone-deep intolerance for looking away from things that are wrong. I have been vocal and campaign-involved ever since. That is not a recent position. It goes back two decades.

Since then I have worked at the intersection of causes, communications, and the stubborn belief that the world can be moved. I pioneered SMS, internet, and DRTV fundraising in India. I worked on campaigns that helped pass the Right to Education Act, pushed government spending on child healthcare into national discourse, and helped bring over a lakh donors to nonprofits across India. During a pandemic, I drove 25,000 kilometres across this country because traditional approaches weren't working and someone had to go.

I am an autodidact and a polymath. None of what I do was formally taught. All of it has been in service of something. I am label-averse — the question of whether I am an activist or a businessman is a false choice. I prefer the backroom to the spotlight. I live simply, keep my needs small, and keep going. I am a social drinker and like well cooked meats — a detail I mention not to provoke, but because honesty about who you are is part of a principled life.

Early Life

My family is from Saharanpur in UP. I grew up in Ahmedabad. I left for Bombay the day my 10th standard exams finished — my uncles ran a darkroom and I had been learning filmmaking since I was 15. So there was no gap and no plan B. Just the work.

The first years in Bombay were hard. Darkroom work without AC. Long hours. No real friends — just colleagues. I built a reputation for solving edge-case problems that others couldn't. That reputation became the foundation for everything that came after.

Real community only came in 2002, when I joined SPRAT — the Society for Promoting Rationality in Ahmedabad. I found other misfits there. People who questioned things without needing you to pretend. It was the first place I felt genuinely at home. A few years later, Greenpeace deepened that — a rare environment where the people around you were both competent and principled, and where I became fully myself professionally.

Road Ashram
October – November 2020

Road Ashram

During the pandemic, three of us drove across India — 25,000 kilometres through 30 states and union territories in 54 days. The pandemic had turned from a health crisis into a humanitarian catastrophe and traditional approaches weren't reaching people who needed help.

So we got in a car and went. Along India's borders, through the Northeast, into every corner of a country that was hurting. Funds raised went to Give India for food relief. Stories were documented and shared. The road was the ashram — it kept us honest, close to the reality, far from abstraction.

Civic Work

Over recent years I have been voluntarily advising opposition parties on strategy, communications, and campaign organising. I have never been paid a single rupee for any political work in my life. I don't work for parties — I work for democracy and for people. That distinction matters.

I may face prosecution for this. I am not afraid. An active citizenry is essential for a functioning democracy — and if more people did what I do, as volunteers, without commercial interest, we would be in a better place as a country.

Don't make me a hero. Get involved yourself. Ask real questions. Be vocal when you see injustice. We need a million Ahmers — not one martyr.

What I Think
01

On religion

I read every major religion and philosophy honestly. I found none of them logically coherent. I am an atheist. Organised religion causes more harm than good. Gujarat 2002 is the evidence I witnessed personally.

02

On India's young and poor

The demographic dividend has been squandered. Joblessness, discrimination, shrinking civic space. AI will take the white-collar jobs that gave the first generation a way out. India needs to build for self-sufficiency — gram swaraj, not startup mythology.

03

On action

Being angry doesn't help. Neither does being broken. The only thing that moves anything is showing up and doing the work — slow, unglamorous, persistent. I survive like a cockroach. That is enough.

04

On states and people

States are by nature repressive and controlling. When their demands are grounded in human rights and liberal values, I will always stand with people over states. Collective punishment is absolutely wrong — no context justifies it.

05

On markets and billionaires

I am pro-market, not free market. Monopolies should not exist. Neither should billionaires. Both are market failures, not market successes. The current relationship between large Indian conglomerates and political power is one of the country's most serious structural problems.

06

On free speech

Strong default for free speech. The line is hate speech and incitement — not discomfort, not offence, not satire. Memes and jokes are not the problem. India should act like a mature democracy, not treat criticism as sedition.

07

On education and healthcare

Both are universal public goods — fully funded, non-discriminatory, one system for everyone. The current multi-tier education system is a caste system in disguise. RTI promised the right. The system didn't deliver the reality.

08

On participation

If I have one message it is this: participate. Not protest, not donate, not vote specifically — participate. An informed, active citizenry is the only thing that keeps democracy working. It is your future at stake.

09

On caste and solidarity

I travelled through UP in 2021-22 and saw caste discrimination in its daily, unremarkable form. The normalisation of inequality is more dangerous than the inequality itself. I stand in full solidarity with Dalits and the downtrodden everywhere.

10

On hope

Despite everything, I have not lost hope in the people of this country. India is largely a kind and open society — it is being degraded, not destroyed. Change is inevitable. The people will win.

Making money matters — you need to survive. But if it's the only thing you're doing, you aren't reaching your real potential. A life spent purely in self-interest isn't a life well lived.

Change something. Leave a mark. Not everyone needs to be an activist — but a principled life is important. The smaller your needs, the harder it is for anyone to compromise you.
